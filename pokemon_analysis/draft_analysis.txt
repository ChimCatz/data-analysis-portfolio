Pokemon has always been a major anime for me. As a kid I first discovered Pokemon through Pokemon Emerald and since then I got hooked. I played the mainline Gen 3 Pokemon games such as Emerald, Sapphire, FireRed, Leaf Green, before proceeding to newer gen leading up to the NDS Pokemon games (Black, White, Black 2, White 2, HeartGold and SoulSilver) and eventually to the newest Switch games (Sword/Shield, Scarlet/Violet, Legends: Arceus). Now with the explosive popularity of Pokemon Legends: ZA, what better way to honor my childhood game than to study it deeper and analyze it through the eyes of an analyst.

Using a public dataset compiled by generous and diehard people and open-sourced on Kaggle, I am now working with a dataset covering 1,025 Pokédex IDs and 1,207 total rows from Generation 1 through Generation 9. The final cleaned dataset includes alternate forms, Mega variants, paradox forms, and additional numeric fields such as experience growth, height, weight, base egg steps, base happiness, and capture rate.

As a long-time Pokemon player there are questions I had in the back of my mind and through this updated dataset I am able to answer them more completely.

SECTION 1

The first order of business are the Pokemon types. Like rock-paper-scissors, each type has its own weaknesses and strengths. But it is not that easy: Pokemon also has a concept called double typing where some Pokemon have two types instead of one, making the matchup matrix more complicated and exciting.

Using Python libraries pandas, numpy, and matplotlib inside VS Code, I generated an 18x18 type matchup matrix and populated it with defensive damage multipliers.

[type-match-matrix.png]

With these data we can answer the following questions:

Q1: From Generation 1 to 9, which single typing is the MOST and LEAST common?
Most common: Water — 81 single-type Water Pokemon.
Least common: Flying — 4 single-type Flying Pokemon.

Q2: From Generation 1 to 9, which double typing is the MOST and LEAST common?
Most common: Normal/Flying — 31 Pokemon.
Several double-type combinations appear only once in this dataset. Examples include Fairy/Ice, Electric/Psychic, Dragon/Fairy, Bug/Ghost, Steel/Water, Normal/Water, Ghost/Ice, Electric/Fire, and Fire/Steel.

Q3: Which single typing has the best offensive capability?

Fighting and Ground are tied for the best offensive single type, each with 5 super-effective matchups. Fighting is strong against Normal, Ice, Rock, Dark, and Steel. Ground is strong against Fire, Electric, Poison, Rock, and Steel. Both types provide excellent offensive coverage, but they succeed in different matchups.

Q4: Which single typing has the best defensive capability?

Steel is the best defensive single type with a defensive score of 10. It resists 11 attacker types and has only 3 weaknesses, making it one of the strongest defensive profiles in the game.

Q5: Which single typing has the worst offensive capability?

Normal is the worst offensive single type because it has zero super-effective matchups. Normal attacks are never the best option for exploiting opponent weaknesses and are completely ineffective against Ghost.

Q6: Which single typing has the worst defensive capability?

Ice is the worst defensive single type with a defensive score of -3. It has only one resistance and four major weaknesses: Fire, Fighting, Rock, and Steel.

Q7: Which double typing Pokemon has the best offensive capability?

Several double-type combinations are tied for the best offensive coverage with 7 super-effective matchups. Examples include Grass/Dark, Grass/Ice, Grass/Psychic, Rock/Fighting, and Rock/Psychic.

Q8: Which double typing Pokemon has the worst offensive capability?

Several double-type combinations have only a single super-effective matchup. Examples include Bug/Steel, Dark/Ghost, Dark/Poison, Electric/Water, Normal/Ghost, and Water/Ground.

Q9: Which double typing Pokemon has the best defensive capability?

Fairy/Steel and Steel/Ghost are the strongest defensive combinations, each with a defensive score of 11. These combinations resist the most attacker types and are among the hardest defensive builds to break.

Q10: Which double typing Pokemon has the worst defensive capability?

Grass/Dragon and Ice/Psychic are tied for the weakest defensive score at -4. These combinations suffer from multiple strong weaknesses and limited resistance options.

Q17: Are Pokemon with higher Defense usually slower?

Pokemon with higher Defense are not usually slower based on these data. The Pearson correlation between Defense and Speed is -0.0271 while the Spearman correlation is 0.0300, which means the relationship is extremely close to zero and effectively very weak. Because one correlation is slightly negative and the other is slightly positive, the dataset does not support a clear pattern that high-defense Pokemon are usually slower.

SECTION 3

Not all Pokemon are the same that is why apart from the Typing and Stat Totals, more hidden information are the classification, experience_growth, capture_rate, and many more. These information have little significance in the mainline gameplay but it is still important in some sense.


Q20: Do Legendary and Mythical Pokemon generally have lower capture rates?

Legendary and Mythical Pokemon do generally have lower capture rates. The mean capture rate is 99.67 for normal Pokemon, 16.65 for Legendary Pokemon, and 9.50 for Mythical Pokemon. This shows that official class is strongly connected to capture difficulty, with Legendary and Mythical Pokemon being much harder to catch on average.

Q21: Do Legendary and Mythical Pokemon generally require more experience to reach Level 100?

Legendary and Mythical Pokemon do generally require more experience growth. The mean experience growth is 1,046,900.35 for normal Pokemon, 1,250,000.00 for Legendary Pokemon, and 1,224,648.00 for Mythical Pokemon. This means Legendary Pokemon usually follow the slowest growth curve, while Mythical Pokemon are also much closer to that higher experience requirement than normal Pokemon.

Q22: Is capture rate strongly related to experience growth?

Capture rate and experience growth are not strongly related on their own. The Pearson correlation is -0.2512 and the Spearman correlation is -0.4004, so the relationship is weak to moderate and negative. This means Pokemon that need more experience growth tend to have lower capture rates, but the relationship is not strong enough to explain capture difficulty by experience growth alone.

Q23: Is official class a stronger signal than raw correlation?

Official class is a stronger signal than raw numeric correlation here because the group differences are much clearer than the overall correlation between capture_rate and experience_growth. Normal Pokemon have much higher capture rates on average, while Legendary and Mythical Pokemon have much lower capture rates and higher experience requirements. This suggests that the category of the Pokemon explains the pattern better than the numeric relationship alone.
